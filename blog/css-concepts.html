CSS Concepts

This week's blog assignment is a good opportunity for me to learn the difference between inline and inline-block, as I have no idea what the difference is. Both are inline positioning elements that allow you to fill the browser or a container's width. For the layman, that means these are pieces of code that let you place content side-by-side, wherever you want, and the content will stretch to fill the whole screen or however far you want. But what is the difference?

Well, it's a few things. But it turns out it's pretty simple. Turns out, an inline-block element is inline, but behaves as a block. But what does that mean?

Inline elements allow content to be displayed side-by-side, they can have left and right margins/padding but not top and bottom, and other elements can be placed to their left and right. However, they can't have width or height.
Block elements can have all margins and always have a line break after the block element. They can have width and height, but no content can be placed to their left or right.

So inline-block often gives you the best of both worlds. You can have other elements side-by side, all margins and padding, and height and width.

But that doesn't mean you would always want to use inline-block. Inline-block is can be complex overkill for simpler elements, like navigation bars, footers and headers. However, if you had, say, a gallery of pictures of varying sizes that you'd like to be side-by-side and the same size, inline-block would be the way to go.
